## **Chapter 7 Summary: Christ’s Planet — Discovering Jesus in the Creation**

*Jesus on Every Page, David P. Murray*

Chapter 7 argues that creation itself is not merely a backdrop to redemption but an intentional revelation of Christ. Murray emphasizes that the natural world is structured, sustained, and interpreted through Jesus Christ. Creation is not neutral—it is Christ-centered in its origin, purpose, and ongoing existence.

Murray explains that Christ is present in creation in at least three ways:

1. **Christ as Creator** – All things were made through Him. Creation is not independent of Christ but originates in Him.
2. **Christ as Sustainer** – The universe continues to exist and function because Christ actively holds it together.
3. **Christ as Revealer** – Creation reflects truths about Christ’s nature, wisdom, power, and glory.

Rather than treating creation as a general revelation that vaguely points to God, Murray presses further—creation specifically points to Christ. The beauty, order, and design of the world are not random; they are expressions of the Son’s divine wisdom and authority.

He also highlights that creation prepares the categories needed to understand redemption. Light and darkness, life and death, order and chaos—all these created realities become the language through which salvation is revealed. Without creation, the gospel would lack its foundational vocabulary.

> *“Creation is the stage, the script, and the set of symbols that make redemption intelligible.”* (paraphrased from Murray)

Ultimately, Murray calls readers to read creation Christologically—to see the world not as an end in itself but as a signpost pointing to the glory of Christ.

---

Chapter 7 highlights a profound truth: **creation itself is a revelation of Christ.** It is not merely that God created the world, but that **the Son Himself is the agent, goal, and meaning of creation**. Everything that exists came into being through Him and for Him, which means creation is fundamentally Christ-centered from the very beginning. The world is not neutral—it is shaped by Christ and points back to Him.

> **John 1:3 (NASB1995)**
> *“All things came into being through Him, and apart from Him nothing came into being that has come into being.”*

> **Colossians 1:16 (NASB1995)**
> *“For by Him all things were created… all things have been created through Him and for Him.”*

At the same time, creation does more than originate from Christ—it **reflects who He is**. The light, order, beauty, and life woven into the world are not random features; they are expressions of His character. The brilliance of light echoes His purity, the structure of creation reflects His wisdom, and the abundance of life points to Him as the source of all life. In this way, creation becomes a kind of silent testimony—constantly displaying glimpses of Christ to all who will see.

> **Psalm 19:1 (NASB1995)**
> *“The heavens are telling of the glory of God; And their expanse is declaring the work of His hands.”*

The takeaway is both simple and profound: **to look at creation rightly is to see traces of Christ everywhere—its source, its design, and its purpose.**

## **Chapter 8 Summary – Seeing Christ in All of Scripture**

Chapter 8 shifts the focus from seeing Christ in creation to seeing Christ **in the entirety of Scripture and history**, correcting a common error: reading the Bible in a man-centered way rather than a God-centered way. As emphasized in your highlights, *“the Bible is a book about God”*—and more specifically, it is a book about Christ.

Murray presses the idea that every passage should lead to the question: *What does this reveal about God?* This directly confronts the modern tendency toward anthropocentric teaching. The issue is not that Scripture lacks relevance to human life—but that its primary purpose is to **reveal the character, will, and redemptive plan of God fulfilled in Christ**.

What becomes clear is that Christ is not only present in isolated prophecies, but that **He sovereignly ordered all events, people, and patterns in the Old Testament to reveal Himself**.  This means biblical history is not merely recorded—it is orchestrated. Figures like Joseph are not just moral examples but **living portraits of Christ**, displaying His character in advance:

> *“Joseph’s forgiveness… reflect[s] the spirit of Jesus… one of the most powerful witnesses to Jesus’ forgiving heart.”*

This expands the understanding of Scripture: it is not simply predictive (prophecy), but also **typological and intentional**, where real people and events are shaped by God to reflect the coming Savior.

### **The Gospel Pattern Embedded in Scripture**

One of the strongest contributions of this chapter is the clarity that the Old Testament believers were not without Christ—they believed in Him **in seed form**.  The foundation for this is seen in the earliest promise:

> **Genesis 3:15 (NASB1995)**
> *“And I will put enmity between you and the woman, and between your seed and her seed;
> He shall bruise you on the head,
> And you shall bruise him on the heel.”*

From this single verse, Murray highlights three core truths that unfold throughout Scripture:

* The Messiah will be **a man** (seed of the woman)
* The Messiah will **suffer** (heel bruised)
* The Messiah will **conquer** (head crushed)

These are not later developments—they are embedded from the beginning and progressively revealed across the Old Testament.

This is reinforced by how the New Testament interprets the Old:

> **John 5:46 (NASB1995)**
> *“For if you believed Moses, you would believe Me, for he wrote about Me.”*

> **1 Peter 1:10–11 (NASB1995)**
> *“As to this salvation, the prophets who prophesied of the grace that would come to you made careful searches and inquiries,
> seeking to know what person or time the Spirit of Christ within them was indicating…”*

> **Acts 3:24 (NASB1995)**
> *“And likewise, all the prophets who have spoken, from Samuel and his successors onward, also announced these days.”*

These passages confirm that Christ is not added to the Old Testament—**He is its substance, its message, and its fulfillment**.

### **Personal Theological Reflection**

This chapter sharpens a growing conviction: Scripture cannot be rightly understood unless Christ is seen as its center—**consistently, intentionally, and sovereignly woven throughout every part**. What becomes clear is that God did not merely predict Christ—He **designed all of history, all people, and all events to reveal His Son**.

This aligns deeply with a commitment to *Sola Scriptura* and the understanding that Christ is not only revealed in isolated passages, but that **the entire Bible—from Genesis to Revelation—is unified in Him**. The Old Testament is no longer a collection of stories or moral lessons, but a divinely orchestrated revelation where Christ is progressively unveiled.

There is also a deepened sense of awe in recognizing that this was not reactionary. Just as redemption was purposed *before the foundation of the world*, so too was the revelation of Christ embedded into every layer of Scripture. The same God who spoke creation into existence also ordered history to testify of His Son.

**To read Scripture rightly is to look for Christ—not as an addition, but as the One to whom every passage ultimately leads.**

## **Chapter 9 Summary – Christ’s Presence in the Old Testament**

Chapter 9 establishes that the Son of God did not begin His work at the incarnation, but is the **eternal Son**, fully equal with the Father and the Holy Spirit, actively involved in all of God’s purposes from the beginning. The chapter emphasizes that Christ was not absent in the Old Testament—rather, He was **present, active, and revealing God** long before taking on flesh.

The Son is shown as both **Creator and Sustainer**, not only bringing all things into existence but continually upholding them. This reinforces that all of creation and history are under His authority and direction. But the chapter moves beyond creation to something even more personal: Christ’s **visible and relational presence with His people**.

Through appearances often described as the *“Angel of the Lord”* or the *“Angel of His Presence,”* the Son of God is presented as the One who **reveals God, speaks for God, intercedes, saves, and shepherds His people**. These are not merely symbolic appearances, but real manifestations of the pre-incarnate Christ, functioning in ways that clearly anticipate His later earthly ministry.

One of the clearest examples highlighted is Christ’s presence with Israel in the wilderness, where He is identified as the **spiritual Rock who sustained them**. This demonstrates that even in the Old Testament, God’s people were not only led by God in a general sense—they were being **personally led and sustained by Christ Himself**.

The chapter ultimately shows that the incarnation was not the beginning of Christ’s involvement with humanity, but the **continuation and climax** of a long history of divine presence. The same Christ who walked in Galilee is the One who walked with Israel, appeared to the patriarchs, and carried His people through every stage of redemptive history.

## **Personal Theological Reflection**

This chapter deepens the understanding that Christ is not only the center of Scripture in message, but also in **presence and activity throughout history**. There is a strengthening conviction that the Old Testament is not simply pointing forward to Christ in promise—it is **filled with His active involvement**, His voice, His compassion, and His leadership.

This brings a greater sense of unity to the Bible. The same Jesus who says, *“He who has seen Me has seen the Father,”* is the One who was already revealing the Father long before the incarnation. There is no division between the Old and New Testaments—only a progression from **hidden presence to revealed presence**.

It also produces a deeper awe of Christ’s personal care. He was not distant from His people in earlier times. He was the One who **carried them, sustained them, interceded for them, and remained faithful even when they were not**. This reframes the Old Testament from being a distant historical record into a testimony of Christ’s **ongoing, faithful, and covenantal presence**.

**Christ has always been present with His people—creating, sustaining, revealing, and redeeming—long before He came in the flesh, and continuing forever as the same faithful Savior.**

## **Chapter 10 Christ's Precepts: Discovering Jesus in the Old Testament Law**

**THREE KINDS OF LAW**

As the Westminster Confession of Faith puts it: *“The moral law doth forever bind all, as well justified persons as others, to the obedience thereof.”*

Again, the Westminster Confession explains: Besides this law, commonly called moral, God was pleased to give to the people of Israel, as a Church under age, ceremonial laws, containing several typical ordinances, partly of worship, prefiguring Christ, His graces, actions, sufferings, and benefits; and partly, holding forth divers instructions of moral duties. All which ceremonial laws are now abrogated, under the New Testament.

But when the Messiah came and Israel rejected Him, God sent the Romans in AD 70 to sweep Israel away—together with its special civil laws.

Speaking of Old Testament Israel, the Westminster Confession states, *“To them also, as a body politic, He gave sundry judicial laws, which expired together with the state of that people; not obliging any other now, further than the general equity thereof may require.”*

**AN EXHIBITION OF CHRIST’S CHARACTER**

This is especially true of God. Because all His words are perfectly free of deceit, hypocrisy, or pretense, they are perfectly self-revealing. They tell us who He is.

**JESUS IS SOVEREIGN**  
The Old Testament precepts communicate the sovereign authority of the divine Lawgiver through the awesome signs and wonders that accompanied the giving of the law at Sinai, and through the unambiguous and undebatable manner of their framing. “Thou shalt” and “Thou shalt not” leave no room for debate, adjustment, or compromise.

**JESUS IS HOLY**  
The spotless and beautiful ethics of the moral law, the laws of cleanliness and separation in the ceremonial law, and the separation of Israel from other nations through the civil law—all reveal the holy character of Christ, the perfect Israel of God, as *“holy, harmless, undefiled, separate from sinners.”*

**JESUS IS JUST**  
In the moral law we see Christ’s concern for fairness and equity between God and man, and between man and man. In the penalties attached to the civil law we see that Christ does not just give the law and then show little interest in whether it is kept or not. Rather, the penalties show His passion for justice and redress.
